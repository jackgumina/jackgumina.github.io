PROBLEM

Bay View Beer needed an online presence to legitimize its brand and communicate its mission. In addition, Bay View Beer needed a space where users could sign up to attend events, so the company could more easily track the numbers.

SOLUTION

I designed a website that sells the company’s values, story, and beer. The website also allows for administrators to add brewery events, and users to sign up for these events.

DISCOVERY & RESEARCH

I developed a survey to learn more about how users interact with craft beer websites. I wanted to know which features users considered important and which other websites users liked. This would help me focus on the most important functionalities.

55% of respondents said they visit brewery websites. For the respondents that visited brewery websites, the top reasons for doing so were viewing the tap list and upcoming events. This gave me a better idea for what to incorporate into the MVP.

I analyzed Sierra Nevada (Chico, CA) and Surly Brewing Company (Minneapolis, MN). I chose these breweries to analyze based on:

  1. Survey respondents’ favorite brewery websites
  2. Conversations with the founders of Bay View Beer Company on their vision of an effective website
  3. My own research on amazing brewery website design

Sierra Nevada's website is easy to navigate with a clear navigation bar and links. The About page is well laid out in a unique timeline design. The website uses typography well and has plenty of engaging content. Sometimes, type can be hard to read, especially when laid over bright images. The paragraphs do not always follow the Bringhurst rule. An opportunity would be to make some paragraphs more readable by giving them a simpler background and creating better line length.

Surly's dark color scheme and unique typography match its prickly, ironic brand. Photos, such as crushed cans instead of full ones, also enhance their brand. Social media links are readily available and call users to interact with the brewery. Sometimes, pages become crowded with lots of high contrast colors and busy photos. This can diminish scanability. An opportunity would be to improve navigation by creating more empty space and reducing unnecessary content.

STORIES & FLOWS

The most important user stories would allow users to view a tap list, see upcoming events, read about the brewery’s story, and contact the brewery. This also meant administrative users needed to be able to create and edit new events.

Below are some examples of the highest priority user stories for an MVP:

  • As a user, I want to see what's on tap so I can decide whether to visit the taproom.
  • As a user, I want to see upcoming events so I can plan on attending.
  • As an administrator, I want to create a new event so I can share event info with users.

Medium priority user stories:

  • As an administrator, I want to edit an existing event's info so I can make changes should they arise.
  • As an administrator, I want to delete an event so I can get rid of events created by mistake.
  • As a user, I want to contact the brewery so I can get questions answered that aren't on the site.
  • As a user, I want to read about the brewery's story so I can learn where they came from.

Low priority user stories:

  • As a user, I want to read details about the beer so I educated myself on the beer style.
  • As a user, I want to locate where to buy beer in stores so I can have the beer at home.
  • As a user, I want to find out if the brewery is dog friendly so I know whether I can bring my dog.

I chose the necessary user stories for an MVP and turned them into detailed user flows. These flows outlined the individual pages needed to complete the most important tasks.

WIREFRAMES

Informed by my user flows, I sketched wireframes of each page, and I transferred these sketches to Figma. Not every Figma component matched the sketches identically, and that was okay. There is a practical gap between what I put on paper and the grid system used on Figma. Using a grid kept components consistently sized and sped up the process significantly.

BRANDING AND VISUAL DESIGN

In order to get a direction on the website's visual design, I spoke with the founders of Bay View Beer and a graphic designer they had hired. Together, we created a brand vision statement:

"Bay View Beer isn’t just a place to grab a drink, it’s a team of people who value heritage and tradition but aren’t afraid to blaze their own trail. It’s about breaking from the expected in the name of the craft instead of hiding behind it. At their core, Bay View Beer is about passion and they brew with a bold spirit."

With this vision statement in mind, we collaborated on moodboards and received logo ideas from our graphic designer.

We settled on a logo that we thought allowed for versatility and captured Bay View Beer's vision statement. In particular, we thought the "B" was bold and original, yet traditional looking and could stand on its own.

I followed guidance from the graphic designer on a color palette for the website. I added white and gray to allow the other colors to stand out.

With input from the graphic designer, I chose all capital Trade Gothic Pro, Bold Condensed No. 20 as the brand typeface. Trade Gothic is bold, adventurous, and spirited. It's also readable in small chunks. I chose Lato as the complimentary typeface for its readability and simplicity.

With the mockups complete, I needed to conduct user tests to determine the functionality of the site and discover any issues I may have missed. I created a test script, and had users conduct a few tasks.

From the average user point of view:

  1. Sign up to attend a brewery event
  2. Submit a contact form to the brewery

From the admin point of view:

  1. Create a new brewery event
  2. Edit an event
  3. Delete an event

The users pointed out some problems I may otherwise not have noticed.

All the users were confused that there was no confirmation message after clicking submit. I added a message to the prototype that confirmed the brewery got the message and how they would be in touch.

On the events page, the users were all a little put off by the wording “Book Place” to reserve a spot at an event. I needed to change this to say “Reserve Spot” and make it clear that the user was reserving a spot at the event, not an entire room or private event.
