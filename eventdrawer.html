People use cloud storage products to help plan events. Google Drive to create a guestlist, Pinterest to share ideas, Dropbox to send an important PDF. Why not use a single product to perform all of these tasks? Event Drawer aims to be the one-stop-shop for event planning needs.

PROBLEM

The majority of users have a hard time predicting attendance levels and feel generally disorganized while event planning.

SOLUTION

Event Drawer is a one-stop-shop for event planning and organization. Users can create events (or drawers), upload or create related files within these drawers, invite contacts, and track attendance.

DISCOVERY & RESEARCH

I created a survey to learn more about potential problems users face when using cloud storage products. I hypothesized that people use cloud storage to help with event planning, but I needed to know if an event planning product was worth pursuing. I focused mostly on two key questions:

  1. Do people use cloud storage products to help plan events?
  2. What problems do users face when planning events?

Over 60% of respondents said they used cloud storage products to assist with event planning. The two biggest problems users faced when planning events were keeping track of attendance levels and feeling generally disorganized and overwhelmed. This survey informed me to create a product that would:

  1. Allow users to track attendance
  2. Keep all event-related info in a single, easy-to-access space

I analyzed Google Drive, Dropbox, and Pinterest, which all allow for easy file creation and collaboration. However, users would have to get creative in their use of each product to effectively use them as event planners. Why not provide a product that was specifically tailored to this purpose?

I created the following user personas:

JAMES

  • Social young professional
  • 25 years old
  • Madison, WI

James often makes plans with friends on the weekends. He makes a decent salary for a person his age, but he is by no means wealthy. He considers himself in line with his peer group of 25 year old college grads.

MOTIVATIONS

James often attends concerts and weekend getaways with friends. Keeping track of attendance and payments can be a nightmare, and nobody really wants to take charge. James thinks an event planning app could help solve this. He also wants a go-to place for photos, so his friends don’t have to text him a bunch of pictures.

GOALS

  1. Keep track of event attendance
  2. Make sure everyone pays equal
  3. Have a go-to area for photos

FRUSTRATIONS

  1. Friends not paying equally for an event
  2. Receving dozens of texts with pictures after a weekend

“It can be a challenge balancing social and professional life, but I guess that’s part of the fun of being young!”

GRETCHEN

  • Middle-aged empty nester
  • 54 years old
  • San Diego, CA

Gretchen has been a dental hygienist for 30 years. She has three children who are out of the house. She and her husband have downsized to a condo, and Gretchen has found the adjustment difficult. She also has a lot of free time. Gretchen and her husband can afford a few vacations per year.

MOTIVATIONS

Gretchen has been adjusting to life without kids at home. In order to take her mind off of it, she plans more vacations with friends. Gretchen believes an app could be useful in keeping track of relevant documents between friends. Right now, there is a lot of back-and-forth email, which can get overwhelming.

GOALS

  1. Have all vacation documents all in one place
  2. Easily share with newcomers who decide to join the trip
  3. Post photos to remember the vacation

FRUSTRATIONS

  1. Receiving too many emails when planning a vacation
  2. Having to text her friends to get photos to show her friends

“I miss my kids! But I do love all this free time.”

STORIES & FLOWS

The most important user stories would allow for a single space to create, store, and share all event-related information. They would also allow for tracking attendance. Below are some examples of high priority user stories.

  • Sign Up
  • Create a new event folder or “drawer”
  • Invite other users to view or edit a folder
  • Accept a shared folder
  • Create, view, and edit an attendance list

I turned user stories into detailed user flows which outlined individual pages needed to complete the most important tasks.

WIREFRAMES

Informed by my user flows, I sketched wireframes of each page, and I transferred these sketches to Figma. Not every Figma component matched the sketches identically, and that was okay. There is a practical gap between what I put on paper and the grid system used on Figma. Using a grid kept components consistently sized and sped up the process significantly.

USER TESTING ROUND 1

I exhausted the ability to improve the wireframes through my own eyes. I needed the help of real users to discover more improvements. During testing, I had each user complete three tasks:

  1. Sign up
  2. Create a new event folder and create one document within it
  3. Move one document from one event folder to another

Overall, users were able to complete the tasks. However, they did offer some suggestions for improvements.

Not all buttons were consistently sized, which sometimes confused users. Was there a difference in importance between the large and small buttons? Did they all function the same? I needed to make all buttons uniform.

When signing up with Google, the users did not know where the verification code would be sent. I needed to clear this up.

When moving files from one drawer to another, the “move” button did not stand out clearly to users. This reminded me that I needed to give the most important elements on the screen priority in the visual hierarchy. I thought the best way to do this would be with the use of color in my high fidelity mockups.

BRANDING AND VISUAL DESIGN

One of the problems users faced when planning events was feeling stressed and overwhelmed. I wanted to induce a feeling of happiness about the event itself, and not the stress of planning it. I came up with four core brand adjectives.

  1. Stress-free
  2. Homey
  3. Comfortable
  4. Fun

In line with my brand adjectives, I chose a warm color palette.

I was initally torn between two brand typefaces -- Berkshire Swash and Yeseva One. Berkshire Swash’s curves and movement give off a sense of fun. Yeseva One still seemed fun, but a bit more readable and sleek. Preference testing showed me 83% of users preferred Yeseva One.

For a logo, I thought a drawer could be homey and fun-looking.

When creating hi-fi mockups, I realized my drawer handle was too detailed for a small scale. I simplified it.

One of the biggest challenges during the visual design process was the use of color throughout the site. I began with a mahogany background along with a cream color instead white.

Through preference testing and discussions with the design team, I came to the conclusion that my background was too dark. It took focus away from the most important elements on the page, such as buttons and text. In addition, the cream color looked dated. I went with white instead to give the site a cleaner and more modern feel. I also lightened the drawers.

USER TESTING ROUND 2

In my second round of usability tests, I had users complete 6 tasks.

  1. Sign up
  2. Create a new event folder or “drawer”
  3. Create a photo album and upload a photo
  4. View an event’s attendance list
  5. Navigate to the inbox and accept an invitation
  6. Sign out

I also had users comment on the brand and feel of the site. How did it make them feel? What was the brand conveying to them? Overall, users were able to complete each of the tasks. They also said the brand felt fun and friendly. However, the site was far from perfect. Users were able to point to improvements that I may not have otherwise noticed.

Input fields were inconsistent and too tall. This confused users because they were unsure if some fields were buttons or not. I needed to shorten the input fields and make them uniform.

On the inbox page, users the messages looked like buttons instead of mail items in an inbox. Also, why were some red and some white? (My idea was to have unread messages be red).

I squared off these elements and reduced their vertical spacing to make them appear more like an email inbox. I also made them white and gray instead of red in order to distinguish them from buttons. I added icons to more clearly display which messages were read and unread.

FINAL THOUGHTS

I had a lot of fun designing a product prototype that could help users with the stress of event planning. Keeping users in mind throughout the entire process was essential. Without their input, I would have missed key improvements on my initial ideas. By listening to my users problems and concerns, I was ultimately able to create something that would be effective in making their lives a little bit easier.
